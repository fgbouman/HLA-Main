Apostille

The Apostille (Hague Convention Abolishing the Requirement for Legalisation for Foreign Public Documents, 1961) provides a standardised method for authenticating documents, including tracking the competent authorities allowed to do the authentication in the same way within countries notaries authenticate the signatures on documents. Examples of designated competent authorities are embassies, ministries, courts or (local) governments. For example, in the United States, the Secretary of State of each state and his or her deputies are usually competent authorities. In the United Kingdom, all apostilles are issued by the Foreign and Commonwealth Office in Milton Keynes.

The types of documents approved for apostille are:

  1. Court documents
  2. administrative documents (e.g. civil status documents)
  3. notarial acts
  4. official certificates which are placed on documents signed by persons in their private capacity, such as official certificates recording the registration of a document or the fact that it was in existence on a certain date and official and notarial authentications of signatures.

Articles of Incorporation

Articles of incorporation, also referred to as the Certificate of incorporation, the Corporate Charter or Memorandum and Articles of Incorporation (M&A), act as a charter to establish the existence of a corporation. In the U.S. this, along with the more complete bylaws establish the governance of the corporation. In commonwealth countries the Article of Incorporation generally fulfill the same function as the bylaws in the U.S. The M&A (Memorandum & Articles of Incorporation) collectively act in the same way in most Commonwealth countries as the Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws in the U.S.

Asset Protection

Asset Protection is a term used to describe the concept of legally transferring your assets into a legal entity that will protect your assets from being easily seized by third parties such as relatives, customers, employees, the general public, criminals or governments.

Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI>

In order to reduce tax evasion by implementing a transparent tax reporting system, the Common Reporting Standard (CRS) builds on the US Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), a US tax reporting requirement for non-U.S. banks and extends it to all countries. AEOI, takes the CRS and makes the reporting of the information between countries automatic. That is to say, the receiving country does not need to request the information, it is automatically sent every year.

Bank Secrecy

In the past, a legal principle in some jurisdictions under which banks were not allowed to provide to authorities personal and account information about their customers unless certain conditions applied (for example, a criminal complaint had been filed). In some cases, additional privacy was provided to beneficial owners through the use of numbered bank accounts or otherwise. Bank secrecy was prevalent in certain countries such as Switzerland, Lebanon, Singapore and Luxembourg, as well as offshore banks and other tax havens under voluntary or statutory privacy provisions. In the past few years actions, first by the U.S. and subsequently by the OECD has filled privacy laws with as many holes as Swiss Cheese. While many of the privacy laws are nominally still in place, most jurisdictions are going to be sharing data. Where formerly it generally took corruption on the part of an insider to pierce the wall of secrecy, global sharing of financial data will open the floodgates to hackers of every stripe. Bank secrecy, such as it was, is dying and will soon be gone.

Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS)

Tax avoidance strategies that exploit gaps and mismatches in tax rules to artificially shift profits to low or no-tax locations. Over 100 jurisdictions, including all of the popular offshore jurisdictions, are now working together to eliminate profit shifting. more...

Bearer Shares

Bearer share certificates have no owner's name imprinted and are the property of the person holding them. Even if available, Bearer Shares should be avoided as a general rule because their use is considered to be highly suspicious in virtually all cases. Most jurisdictions no longer allow bearer shares.

By-laws

The By-laws, or in some countries, the "Articles of Association", are rules the corporation creates for its shareholders, officers, and directors. These rules are adopted by the Board of Directors as one of the first organizational steps in setting up a corporation. By-laws are usually maintained internally but may be publicly filed if requested.

Foreign Accounts Tax Compliance Act (FATCA)

FATCA requires that foreign financial Institutions and certain other non-financial foreign entities report on the foreign assets held by their U.S. account holders or be subject to withholding on withholdable payments.

Because the U.S. is the global financial center, virtually no bank that wants to engage in significant international business can avoid complying with FATCA. Accordingly U.S. citizens and companies now have their foreign earnings fully disclosed to the U.S.

Human Development Index

The Human Development Index (HDI) is a composite statistic used to measure the development level of a country. Its components are:

  1. Life expectancy at birth. (LEI) Ranges from 0 at 20 years life expectancy to 1 at 85 years of life expectancy.
  2. Education (EI) as a composite of the mean level of education compared to the goal level with a score of 15 years of education and an advanced education goal of 18 years.
  3. Gross Annual Income (II) per capita with $75,000 being 1 and $100 being 0.

The Human Development Index is then calculated as the geometric mean of the three numbers above.

Index of Economic Freedom

The Index of Economic Freedom (IEF) is a composite indicator calculated by the Wall Street Journal and the Heritage Foundation. Because the numbers are not quantitatively derivable in most cases or the numbers used are subject to argument, ideological bias inevitable in the calculation. However, it is the best source available for making comparisons between countries.. The components are as follows:

  1. Property Rights. Stronger rights get higher scores.
  2. Freedom from Corruption as calculated by Transparency International
    more...
  3. Fiscal Freedom. An index based on the top marginal tax rate for individuals and corporations as well as the total tax burden as a share of GDP.
  4. Government spending as a percentage of GDP. The calculation here assumes that the smaller the government, the better it is with zero government being the best of all.
  5. Business Freedom. A Heritage Foundation index composed of the cost in time and money to open, license and close a business.
    more...
  6. Labor FreedomThe less security labor has, the higher the score. These are the components:
    • Ratio of minimum wage to the average value added per worker,
    • Hindrance to hiring additional workers,
    • Rigidity of hours,
    • Difficulty of firing redundant employees,
    • Legally mandated notice period, and
    • Mandatory severance pay.
  7. Monetary Freedom An index calculated using three years of inflation rates and the severity of price controls.
  8. Trade Freedom An index using a combination of trade-weighted tariffs and non-tariff barriers. more...
  9. Investment Freedom The freedom to move investment within and through a country's borders.
  10. Financial Freedom The degree to which the financial sector is able to act on its own behalf without interference from the government. The following criteria are used:
    1. The extent of government regulation of financial services,
    2. The degree of state intervention in banks and other financial firms through direct and indirect ownership,
    3. The extent of financial and capital market development,
    4. Government influence on the allocation of credit, and Openness to foreign competition.
    more...

Intellectual Property (IP)

Intangible property of value. IP is commonly used to shift profits to low-tax/no-tax jurisdictions. The rights to the patent are sold to the midshore or offshore company for a nominal feel which then bills the original holder of the patent for using the IP embedded in the patent (or trademark, design, song etc.).

Jurisdiction Types

Jurisdictions are now classified into three types rated on their relation to the individual using them and to their specific characteristics. e.g. for a person living in Ireland, Ireland would be an onshore location but for anyone else it would be considered offshore.

In industry parlance the issue is generally more complex as the issues of privacy, bank safety and tax regulations also enter. Hence, although Denmark is offshore relative to Indonesia, its tax structure prevents it from being considered an offshore location. The jurisdiction types and their defining characteristics are as follows:

  • Offshore jurisdictions have small, weak governments, low or no taxes for businesses and individuals not earning income within the country and are home to banks of varying degrees of reliability and safety. In the past they also had strong privacy laws but since the advent of AEOI and FATCA, their privacy and tax haven status has been rapidly eroding.
  • Mid-shore or Midshore jurisdictions may be of any size, but they share certain characteristics:
    • Stable government
    • Good business environment
    • Relatively low business taxes
    • Relatively low income taxes
    • A reluctance to freely share information with other jurisdictions.
  • On-shore or Onshore jurisdiciton is the country that is an individual or business' tax home from the vantage point of living in that jurisdiction. We all live in on-shore locations which may or may not be good places to protect our assets. Generally the safest move for anyone with even a modest amount of assets is to move some portion to a mid-shore (not offshore) jurisdiction. For anyone wanting to operate an international business, this is doubly true.

    • Nominee Director

    A nominee director is an individual or corporate body that is officially registered as the administrator of an offshore corporation instead of its real beneficial owner or manager who thereby remains anonymous. It is common to use the nominee director in conjunction with a nominee shareholder in order to avoid the beneficial owner(s) showing up as owner(s) or as manager(s) of the company.

    The nominee director and shareholders exist to protect the beneficial owner's anonymity. For that reason their function is normally a formality. Nominiees appear to be in charge, but their only function is to sign certain documents, including, but not limited to the minutes of the annual meeting of the board of directors.

    The beneficial owner normally performs all operating functions. In the normal course of events, the nominee provides the beneficial owner(s) with a general power of attorney allowing them to conduct all the affairs of the company.

    Nominee Shareholder

    A nominee shareholder is a person or entity listed as the holder of the securities or shares of a corporation rather than its actual owner. As with nominee directorships, this allows the beneficial owner to remain anonymous. The nominee shareholder may be either a natural person or a corporate body.

    Because the nominee shareholder is referenced in the documents of the company and has the rights associated with the shares, it is necessary to take steps to ensure the rights of the beneficial owner. This is generally solved by means of a Declaration of Trust. The Declaration of Trust is a private contract in which the nominee shareholder acknowledges that the shares are being held on behalf of the beneficial owner, defines the beneficial owner's rights and which sets out the manner in which they will be returned to the owner.

    Offshore Corporation

    A corporation is a body with limited liability as recognized by a country's business law. A corporation has the option to sell shares, the right to sue and be sued, and has perpetual existence. An offshore corporation is one that is established in a low-tax jurisdiction and outside of the owner(s) country of tax residence.

    Profit Shifting

    Through various mechanisms, ways are found to have the profits of a business appear at a low or no tax jurisdiction

    Re-invoicing

    Re-invoicing is a form of profit-shifting by using a company in an actual or synthetic tax haven that will act as an intermediary between an onshore business and its customers outside its tax home. Some or all profits are shifted so that they can be accrued at the offshore or midshore corporation. Similar structures work for both exporters and importers.

    Registered Agent

    A Registered Agent is required by State and National governments to provide an assigned representative at a known address to receive all service of process (legal notices) on a corporation's behalf. Upon receipt, the Registered Agent forwards these documents to the beneficial owner in the manner specified in their agreement.

    Registered Shares

    Registered share certificates indicate the name of the owner on the document. Depending on the jurisdiction of incorporation, the name of the owners may not appear on a public registry.

    Shelf Companies

    Shelf Companies are ready-made, never used corporations that have been created in case insufficient time is available to create a corporation from scratch or there is a desire to have a corporation that has a certain amount of maturity.

    Synthetic Tax Haven

    A "Tax Haven" created by moving assets and ownership between two or more companies in order to create a situation where the tax on the asset or income will be lower than if it had been recognized in its "correct" tax home.
