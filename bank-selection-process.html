How we Vet Banks

We once had the unfortunate experience of needing to make an unanticipated international wire transfer. We used one of our bank accounts in a major global bank to wire to a smaller bank in another country. The smaller bank didn't have a SWIFT number. Nor did its correspondant bank where it actually kept its money. (Even banks need to keep some of their money someplace else for various reasons.) Their correspondant bank had its own correspondant bank that did have a SWIFT number. Unfortunately, the sending bank's system couldn't cope with this extra layer of complexity. Most people would have been out of luck at that point.

 

We were fortunate to have another bank in the same country as the small bank so we could handle the transfer in two steps, but how many people are able to count on having a backup account available as we did? We'd have saved time, money and aggravation if from the outset we had been more careful in selecting the small bank and ensuring that it would be easy to wire money to it from anywhere. Lesson learned.

 

This is just one example of the unexpected things that can happen when you are banking internationally. Simple problems like that can cause more than mere aggravation. They can lose business for you or even cause you to lose the money you've placed in the bank.

 

We have encountered situations in third world countries where moving money was even more problematic. Forewarned is forearmed. But, in the grand scheme of things, that is one of the lesser banking problems you can run into. Some are much bigger and are out of your contol once they start to happen.

 

By now just about everyone knows what it means when a government "bails out" a bank... the government puts up the money to keep the bank in business, usually establishing very strong controls on the bank or even taking outright ownership of it. but what about a "bail-in?"

 

How Bank Bail-ins Work

If the jurisdiction you choose is financially weak and for some reason, perhaps another Global Financial Crisis (GFC) your bank runs into cash flow problems, rather than the government providing a bail-out, it may require that YOU bail out the bank using the money you have deposited there. It has happened in the past and it is more likely to happen in the future as the EU how has official rules that include bail-ins. So these days, when you make a deposit to a bank, you may suddenly find you are lending money to the bank rather than the bank lending money to you. At least in the EU there is a pecking order to this and you wouldn't be at the top of the list: 1. Shareholders, then 2. Bondholders and finally 3. Depositors. The trouble is, in crisis conditions they can move through 1 and 2 very quickly... long before you can get your money out. Of course, if you are choosing to bank in an offshore jurisdiction such as Cyprus, you may find that you come first on the government's list of whose money to take.

 

Steps to Vet a Bank

First: Determine which jurisdiction to use:

 

This is in the process of becoming much easier as a result of OECD efforts to ensure that they can collect all the taxes they are due. Nevertheless, in order to make a proper selection, you should be familiar with CRS, AEOI and BEPS as they apply to each jurisdiction AND the jurisdiction(s) in which you are a tax resident.

 

You must also examine the financials of the countries you have shortlisted and their banking laws. (Some require no more than the payment of a license fee and a small paid-up capital amount and make no ongoing reporting demands or perform periodic audits of the banks. You will not be able to obtain enough information to vet the banks properly.)

 

Determine the international banking relations of the jurisdiction. In addition to meeting the CRS/AEOI & BEPS requirements, the country must also comply with anti-money laundering requirements if it is to be able to move your money freely when you need it.

 

If the country issues its own currency, check out the convertability situation. If you intend to open a foreign currency account there, verify that when the time comes to retrieve your foreign currency, it will actually be available and not be sequestered in the country's reserves.

 

Second: Check out financial publications.

 

Some of the good sites to check out are: Euromoney, Global Banking & Finance, Global Finance Magazine & Forbes Magazine. For economics information of all sorts such as bank solvency or the Bahamas, go here.

 

We use these publications and those of various central banks as a starting point for our investigations. Given the huge number of banks in the world, it isn't practical to try to vet them all. These publications provide some preliminary screening and also give you a feel for what is going on in the world of finance. Even after you decide on a bank, you could do worse than to read a couple of these on a weekly basis as time permits. You'll have a better understanding of how bankers view the world if you do.

 

Third: Shortlist your choices.

 

Now that you have figured out some stable countries and selected a few banks in each of them, now it is time to see if the banks are actually safe to put your money in... or at least how safe they were when the data was gathered.

 

The most important basic criteria are those of solvency and liquidity. The first determines the likelihood that they'll be able to remain in business. The second determines the likelihood that you'll be able to get your money back in periods of crisis. For this information you are going to need to get your hands on the bank's balance sheet. The more recent, the better as things have a way of changing quickly. You are going to need the bank's balance sheet to get this information. If you can't find the bank's balance sheet, DON'T BANK THERE. Not publishing a balance sheet doesn't mean the bank is good, but it definitely means you can't tell if it is good or bad. Of course, if you have a billionaire buddy whose family has been banking there for generations, perhaps you'll want to make an exception. But if it fails, don't say we didn't warn you: check out the balance sheet!

 

These are the ratios that we use to determine which banks are and are not acceptable. The information isn't always easily available or completely comparable between banks, even in the same jurisdiction. But they are as good a group of indicators as we are likely to find.

 

Solvency Ratio:

Equity divided by assets.
Try for at least ten percent. If the bank has a ten percent solvency ratio, the likelihood that they'll be relying on you to involuntarily fund the bank is quite low. Higher is better, of course.

 

Liquidity Ratio:

Cash and Cash Equivalents divided by Deposits.
Cash equivalents are things such as U.S.Treasury Bills that can be quickly liquidated. Corporate bonds, debentures and those sorts of things don't count as they take too much time to liquidate and will probably lose value in a crisis. Ten percent should be the minimum acceptable.

We generally exclude fixed time deposits from our deposit calculation as in a crisis there's a fair likelihood that they won't come due for payment. They are normally a small fraction of the deposits and are unlikely to affect your decision.

 

Loan Performance:

Non-performing loans divided by total private loans.
If the bank makes loans (not all banks do, although in conventional banking, that is their primary source of revenue) then the balance sheet should show both the outstanding loans and the amount of non-performing loans. You may occasionally run into a smaller bank where this activity is kept off-balance sheet and they simply provide a percentage for non-performing loans. You can decide whether to believe it or not. We look for a ratio of less than one percent.

 

Derivative Ratio:

Gross Positive Fair Value divided by Tier One Capital.
Derivatives are complex and can be very dangerous. No one completely understands this area of finance, largely because it is to very large part custom-designed for one-off transactions. However, we can unequivocally state that the huge numbers that one sees on alarmist news stories have nothing to do with the actual danger that they represent. Still, they do represent some danger and this must be accounted for.

After all the netting out of the transactions takes place, there are two numbers that remain: the gross positive fair value and the gross negative fair value. The gross negative fair value is the claim that the bank has on other banks and the gross positive value is the claims that other banks have on it. In a crisis the bank may not be able to recover the claims it has on other banks (gross-negative) but should be in good enough shape to pay the banks that have claims on it (gross positive). So we use the gross-positive fair value and measure that against the Tier One Capital of the bank. the number should be larger than one. The larger the better. One of the banks we recommend has a ratio of almost 13:1 and another 5:1. And, of course, many banks do not use derivatives at all.

 

Does this seem like more work than you want to deal with? We can help.

 

So now the easy part is done (!). We have a short list of financially viable banks in financially viable jurisdictions. We still need to check out their services and their helpfulness. In that latter category we will find out if they will accept us. Clearly, if they won't, they fail the helpfullnesss test. We'll assume that you aren't running guns or drugs so you have at least some chance of opening an account, so we'll check out the services first.

For this you'll need to go on each bank's site and see if you can find the services that you want. Even if you can, you should follow up with a phone call to verify that what you see on the Internet is true. Often there are conditions that you cannot or don't want to meet.

 

Do they provide essential Services?

  1. Personal Bank Accounts
  2. Business Bank Accounts
  3. SWIFT Number

    If no SWIFT number, then check deeper. If they use a correspondant to handle their international inbound transfers, check to see that it is no more than one.

  4. Physical office with staff in the country in which they operate. Some banks operate out of a mailbox. Beware!
  5. Easy phone contact.
  6. Multiple staff proficient in your language
  7. Deposit Guarantee

    Deposit guarantee plans vary by country. Participation is usually mandatory, if it exists, but not always. Don't make assumptions if a deposit guarantee is important to you.

How helpful are they?

  1. Email the bank to verify the data that you have collected. Note the date and time you mailed it and the date/time you received the reply.
  2. Call them on the phone and ask the questions once again to whoever answers. Ensure you take their names and write them down. Be sure to get the proper spelling. Then ask them about their solvency ratio or capital ratio and about their liquidity. Then ask to speak to a supervisor. Ask her/him the same questions after jotting down the name. Write down any problem areas or gaps in their knowledge.
  3. Call again the following day and try to talk to the same persons. Go over the answers and make certain that you got them right. By this point you aren't checking the answers, you are checking the attitude of the staff. (I once called my banker on his cell phone as he was hanging on the side of a cliff in the Alps. He was still helpful and pleasant. It wasn't until I was finished with my business that he mentioned his situation. Now that's a good attitude.)
  4. Think of a problem that directly relates to your business that could come up and ask how they would handle it. Write down the answer.

 

When you complete this step, compare your findings and select the banks you want to deal with. You can now be somewhat confident that you have a bank to deal with that will meet your needs.
